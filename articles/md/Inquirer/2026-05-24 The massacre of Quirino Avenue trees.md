### Editorial

# The massacre of Quirino Avenue trees

#### Philippine Daily Inquirer / 05:15 AM May 24, 2026

[https://opinion.inquirer.net/191897/the-massacre-of-quirino-avenue-trees](https://opinion.inquirer.net/191897/the-massacre-of-quirino-avenue-trees)

---

Manila folks woke up this week to the unsightly spectacle of tree stumps covered with tarpaulins lining up the sidewalk of Quirino Avenue, where sprawling trees used to provide cover for pedestrians and motorists from the punishing urban heat.

The tree-cutting of 617 trees to make way for a skyway project has been approved by the Department of Environment and Natural Resources (DENR); 225 trees have been felled so far, including a 50-year-old narra tree.

The undertaking is to give way to the construction of the 40.62-kilometer (km) Southern Access Link Expressway (SALEx) project, another public-private partnership between the Department of Public Works and Highways and San Miguel Corp. (SMC). SALEx will traverse portions of Quirino Avenue, San Marcelino Street, and areas terminating near Roxas Boulevard, where it will connect to the Metro Manila Skyway Stage 3. The Quirino Avenue segment spans 3.97 km and features four elevated lanes.

The DENR has defended the operation, saying it was authorized under a valid permit and that the local government has secured a “certificate of no objection.” The agency also cited Presidential Decree No. 705, PD 953, and other existing forestry and environmental regulations, which allow the cutting or earth-balling of trees when necessary for infrastructure projects, public works, and other development activities.

## More sustainable solution

SMC, it said, is mandated to plant 57,000 seedlings in Manila in line with the memorandum on seedling replacement uniform ratio to “offset” the trees that were cut. This justification, however, creates a false sense of ecological balance and glosses over the years that it would take for a tree to fully mature.

Narra—the Philippines’ national tree and scientifically known as Pterocarpus indicus—for example, has been listed by the International Union for Conservation of Nature as an “endangered” species. Planting a sapling elsewhere cannot simply replace a 5-decade-old narra tree capable of absorbing more carbon in a single year.

If the DENR really wanted a more sustainable solution, it should have strictly enforced the earth-balling of the trees to ensure their safe transplanting. Japan employs the “nemawashi method,” a technique that involves meticulously sifting through soil to locate a tree’s root system, which is then wrapped and bound by hand before being moved to a new location. Is this method too complicated for the lazy, short-sighted officials at the DENR, whose idea of development and progress appears to be hinged on gray infrastructure at the expense of green spaces?

## Crime against the environment

The agency may have the legal authority to greenlight this crime against the environment, but this also runs counter to its mandate to conserve, manage, and properly use the country’s environment and natural resources. It proclaims its mission on its website: “To mobilize our citizenry in protecting, conserving, and managing the environment and natural resources for the present and future generations,” but it has instead led in pushing for unsustainable solutions that contribute to further environmental degradation.

The agency needs to be reminded that just because the law allows them to issue a permit doesn’t mean that they should go ahead and do it without weighing the environmental costs. As concerned citizens have suggested, it needs to be audited and the projects it has green lighted reviewed.

Some laws, like Republic Act No. 3571, in fact, prohibit the “cutting, destroying or injuring of planted or growing trees, flowering plants and shrubs or plants of scenic value along public roads, in plazas, parks, school premises or in any other public ground” except when necessary for public safety.

## Feels like hell

The tree-cutting on Quirino Avenue only shows the DENR’s utter disregard and willful ignorance of the impact that it will have on the environment. It also comes on the heels of a new report by the Asean Centre for Energy, noting that extreme heat is accelerating across Southeast Asia. “The region is warming quickly, and the urban heat island effect is intensifying the crisis,” the report published in April states.

Metro Manila already feels like hell at the peak of the dry season, and with global warming, it could only feel like Satan lives among us. The country will soon be made up of pockets of hell if the government sticks to its urban development mindset without environmental preservation.

Almost 400 Quirino trees remain standing—if the chainsaws of the DENR have not cut through them yet—but their massacre could still be prevented through legal remedies, such as the filing for a writ of kalikasan.

Trees not only provide shade, but also act as natural air purifiers, help mitigate floods, and provide habitats for other species. They should never be seen as obstacles to development, but as indispensable assets in creating livable and sustainable cities.
