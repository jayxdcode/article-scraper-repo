# EDITORIAL — Hazing culture

#### The Philippine Star

#### March 7, 2026 | 12:00am

Another hazing death, again attributed to members of the Tau Gamma Phi fraternity. For the past 30 years, the fraternity has gained notoriety for the deaths of neophytes in initiation rites.

Interior Secretary Jonvic Remulla has warned 16 Tau Gamma members to surrender or be subjected to a hot pursuit by the police. Remulla issued the warning following the death of Mark Kenneth Alcedo, 19, a freshman at the Philippine Nautical and Technological Colleges in Dasmariñas, Cavite allegedly following violent hazing last Sunday.

Remulla should also pursue the culpability of Tau Gamma officials. The Anti-Hazing Law passed in 2018 states: “The incumbent officers of the fraternity, sorority or organization concerned shall be jointly liable with those members who actually participated in the hazing.”

It’s been over three decades since Ateneo de Manila University law student Leonardo Villa was killed in initiation rites of the Aquila Legis fraternity. The fraternity network ensconced in the judiciary is suspected to have protected the killers, who were given a slap on the wrist, with several of them even becoming lawyers.

Villa did not receive justice and the fraternity was never sanctioned. The death led to the passage of an anti-hazing law in 1995, Republic Act 8049. But RA 8049 was a farce. It “regulated” hazing and other forms of initiation rites, effectively sanctioning the beastly practice. Not surprisingly, hazing deaths continued.

It took 20 years before the law was fully applied. In 2015, the Supreme Court upheld the 2011 conviction of Alpha Phi Omega members Dandy Dungo and Gregorio Sibal Jr. The Laguna Regional Trial Court sentenced the two to life in prison for the fatal hazing of Marlon Villanueva, a 21-year-old agriculture economics student of the University of the Philippines-Los Baños, in Laguna in January 2006.

And it took another three years before RA 8049 was amended by RA 11053, the Anti-Hazing Law of 2018. This law expressly prohibited hazing and regulated other forms of initiation rites of fraternities, sororities and similar organizations, including those that are community-based.

By that time, the list of fatal initiation rites had grown much longer, involving mostly cadets in military schools. One fraternity also stands out for the highest number of hazing deaths: Tau Gamma Phi. Its fatal initiation rites were conducted not only in Cavite, Remulla’s home province, but also in other parts of the country.

Brutal hazing must be part of the culture of this fraternity. It’s time to apply the full force of the Anti-Hazing Law.
