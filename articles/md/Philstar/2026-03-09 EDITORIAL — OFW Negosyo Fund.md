# EDITORIAL — OFW Negosyo Fund

#### March 9, 2026 | 12:00am

The allocation of P2 billion to help overseas Filipino workers start a business is a welcome development.

Providing access to capital is important, but the business will also need a lot of support to become viable. The OFW Negosyo Fund program must include protection from the many hindrances to doing business in this country, which entrepreneurs from micro to large have complained about for years.

The Department of Trade and Industry announced the launch on March 12 of the OFW Negosyo Fund, which will be managed by the DTI’s Small Business Corp.

It is being rolled out as the conflict widens in the Middle East, home to millions of OFWs. Foreign service officials have warned that the war could last longer than the three to four weeks that US President Donald Trump had originally projected when he ordered the bombing of Iran to change its leadership.

There has been no rush so far among OFWs in the Middle East to return to the Philippines, partly because evacuation is difficult at this time, and partly because many are hesitant to leave their jobs, whose pay they cannot hope to earn in their own country. Because of the risks involved, salaries in the Middle East also tend to rise significantly during times of armed conflict.

But for those who want to return home, the DTI program is a welcome initiative. It allows OFWs to avail themselves of loans ranging from P30,000 to P2 million. Loans of up to P5 million require no collateral, and payment of principal and interest is waived for the first year. Applications can be filed online or at 1,431 DTI Negosyo Centers nationwide.

Assistance must start at the application stage, which should be made as simple as possible while not doing away with due diligence in assessing a business proposal.

Expert mentoring is a must, from getting the business started to keeping it going amid all the challenges – and there are always many in any enterprise.

The OFW-turned-entrepreneur must be protected from the red tape that has plagued the business community, which springs from inefficiency and corruption, from the barangay to the higher levels of government.

Many OFWs have tried their hand at running a business in the country before giving up and deciding to instead seek steady, meaningful employment overseas. The OFW Negosyo Fund program must have a different outcome.
