# EDITORIAL - Don’t you have a  group chat?

#### The Philippine Star

#### March 19, 2026 | 12:00am

Commuters are of course happy, while transport operators and drivers are dismayed and probably wondering if they should just return to selling eggs and vegetables again, as many of them did at the height of the pandemic lockdowns.

This was after President Marcos, in a video statement released to the media yesterday, announced the indefinite postponement of the fare hikes originally scheduled today for most modes of road mass transportation.

Other quarters, on the other hand, saw disorganization in the government’s response, with the President reversing his transport officials practically overnight. This was troubling for those who stressed the need for efficient, decisive responses to a crisis that threatens to become protracted.

Sen. Imee Marcos, the President’s sister, wasn’t the only one asking administration officials yesterday: “Don’t you have a group chat?”

In suspending the fare hikes, which were approved only last Tuesday by the Land Transportation Franchising and Regulatory Board, the President said he wanted to give commuters, notably ordinary workers and students, reprieve from economic woes arising from the Middle East crisis. He did not say it, but higher fares also fuel inflation – consistently the top concern of Filipinos, as shown in multiple surveys.

Drivers and operators of public utility vehicles will instead get more forms of assistance from the government, the President vowed.

Among the expected sources of relief is either a suspension or cut in the excise tax on fuel, which ranges from P10 per liter for gasoline, P6 for diesel, P3 for kerosene and P3 per kilogram of liquefied petroleum gas. The timing of the suspension, however, is up in the air.

Excise taxes are slapped on so-called “sin” products – alcohol, tobacco and vaporized cigarettes – with the proceeds allocated to the Universal Health Care program and the Philippine Health Insurance Corp.

Fuel consumption is deemed as a “sin” supposedly to discourage the use of private cars. Until mass transport facilities improve dramatically, however, this isn’t going to happen. Long before the current fuel crisis, certain groups have been calling for the elimination of the fuel excise tax.

The excise tax is on top of the 12-percent value-added tax on petroleum products, which several groups also want to be either lowered or suspended.

In recent days, traffic has become markedly lighter in Metro Manila as sky-high fuel prices force PUV drivers and private motorists alike to reduce trips or just stay home.

There are no simple solutions to a crisis where the main factors are beyond the nation’s control. But a coordinated response to mitigate the impact of the crisis is indispensable.

A group chat would help.
