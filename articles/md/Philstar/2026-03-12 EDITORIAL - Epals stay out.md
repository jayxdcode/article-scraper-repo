# EDITORIAL - Epals stay out

#### The Philippine Star

#### March 12, 2026 | 12:00am

The government begins distributing this week the fuel subsidy to drivers and micro operators of public utility vehicles, and is finalizing similar assistance for small-scale farmers and fisherfolk.

Funding, according to the government, will be sourced from an unconditional aid program administered by the Department of Social Welfare and Development, the Assistance to Individuals in Crisis Situations.

AICS became controversial because it was used extensively by both local and national politicians for self-promotion, despite repeated avowals by DSWD officials that politicians are not supposed to play a role in the distribution of the agency’s aid programs.

AICS covers a wide range of needs, from medical to wedding, baptism and burial expenses. Politicians routinely posed on stage or displayed streamers with their names and images at the AICS distribution sites or service centers. Politicians endorsed AICS beneficiaries.

In passing the 2026 General Appropriations Act amid the still unresolved corruption scandal involving the national budget and public works, lawmakers ignored public protests and retained their so-called soft pork barrel, led by AICS and the Medical Assistance to Indigent and Financially Incapacitated Persons. To allay public protests, they included an anti-epal provision in the GAA.

This provision, along with the pronouncements of DSWD officials about the depoliticized nature of their aid programs, will be tested in the current fuel crisis, as the government rolls out assistance to the neediest sectors.

Along with close scrutiny of the fuel subsidy distribution, people are also monitoring compliance with the anti-epal circular issued last month by the Department of the Interior and Local Government.

DILG Secretary Jonvic Remulla’s memorandum circular, based on the anti-epal provision in the GAA, prohibits the display of epal materials in public spaces and the presence of politicians and their self-promotional materials at aid distribution activities or service centers.

While compliance with his circular has been reported among LGU officials, materials featuring certain epal senators and their relatives continue to be displayed. These politicians probably believe that they are either above the law or the DILG has no jurisdiction over them.

President Marcos himself reminded all public officials just days ago about the anti-epal policy in the distribution of state-funded aid.

The current fuel crisis will test this policy.
