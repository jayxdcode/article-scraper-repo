# EDITORIAL - New positions, better responses?

#### March 20, 2026 | 12:00am

There’s a presidential adviser for sustainable and resilient communities, and now there’s a special envoy for disaster risk reduction and management.

Both roles are new, accommodating two women who were moved out of the Cabinet. Christina Garcia Frasco was sworn in as resiliency adviser on March 18, days after losing her post as tourism chief.  Maria Antonia Yulo-Loyzaga, who was sacked last year as secretary of the Department of Environment and Natural Resources, took her oath on the same day as special envoy.

With two new special positions plus the DENR secretary, the public should expect top-caliber responses to climate change-related issues and other environmental concerns.

The fuel crisis arising from the Middle East conflict is the most immediate concern at this time. Before this, national attention was focused on the still unresolved budget and flood control corruption scandal as well as the impeachment proceedings against Vice President Sara Duterte.

Climate change, however, poses an existential threat to the country, which experts have described as one of the most vulnerable to the destructive impacts of global warming.

This year’s dry season is just about to start, which could bring extreme heat that is destructive to crops. When the wet season rolls around by midyear, Filipinos will be reminded once again about the failure of flood control interventions, despite the billions in public funds poured into them.

In recent years, the country has seen the deadly and destructive consequences of extreme weather. The development of sustainable and climate-resilient communities, however, has moved slowly.

Environmental destruction continues, even in Metro Manila, where the government has been unwilling or unable to stop massive reclamation activities in the guise of flood control in Laguna de Bay, a major source of clean water and fish.

In November last year, Frasco’s home province of Cebu was devastated by a killer flood spawned by torrential rainfall from Typhoon Tino. A residential hillside property development – Monterazzas de Cebu in Barangay Guadalupe – was among the factors blamed by the public for the extreme flooding. The DENR is still looking into the disaster, wherein Frasco’s hometown of Liloan was among the hardest hit.

Last January, a portion of the Binaliw landfill in Cebu City collapsed. When the search ended about 10 days later, 36 people mostly workers were confirmed killed.

With two new positions created to deal with environmental issues, the public expects a marked improvement in disaster preparedness, mitigation and resilience.
