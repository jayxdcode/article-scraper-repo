# EDITORIAL — ‘Water bankruptcy’

#### The Philippine Star

#### March 22, 2026 | 12:00am

Before conflict in the Middle East pushed fuel prices into the stratosphere and raised the specter of a fuel shortage, the country already lacked another basic commodity: clean water.

Around 40 million or over a third of the country’s population of 115 million lack access to a reliable water supply, according to the Department of Environment and Natural Resources.

As the nation marks World Water Day today, a DENR official said the country faces “water bankruptcy,” with funding constraints limiting the ability of the government to respond to the challenge.

The DENR estimates that about P200 billion is needed to adequately address the problem. Water lack has led to public schools lacking water even for handwashing – a basic requirement for hygiene – and millions of Filipinos still defecating in the open.

Worldwide, today’s observance of World Water Day is focused on giving women greater participation in water management and governance, with the theme, “where water flows, equality grows.”

This year’s edition of the United Nations World Water Development Report shows that 2.1 billion people still lack safely managed drinking water, with the burden heaviest on women and girls.

In many impoverished communities, women and girls spend over three times more hours than men and boys fetching potable water, the UN report said. This leaves the women and girls less time for formal education, livelihood and leisure activities, and deprives women of the opportunity to participate in water management.

The UN report also noted that between 2016 and 2022, the lack of toilets and water for menstrual hygiene made an estimated 10 million adolescent girls worldwide feel ashamed to go to school, work or join social activities. As of 2023, UN Women estimated that over one billion women worldwide lacked access to safely managed drinking water services.

Approximately 1.8 billion people lacked drinking water on-premises as of 2023, when the World Health Organization reported that around 1,000 children under age five died every day due to causes linked to unsafe water, sanitation and hygiene.

This World Water Day, the UN is pushing for equal access to water resources for both men and women, although this problem is not too pronounced in the Philippines. The problem in the country is the lack of access of the general population to potable water. And the problem has long waited for a better response.
